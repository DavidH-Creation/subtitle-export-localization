# Culture Adaptation

Use this file when a line contains culture-locked references that international viewers will not understand without adaptation. The goal is not to explain Chinese culture in subtitles, but to preserve the **scene function** (humor, shock, sympathy, tension) using culturally legible alternatives.

## Core principle

Translate the effect, not the reference. If a viewer needs a footnote to get the joke or feel the sting, the localization has failed.

## Adaptation strategies

Pick one strategy per reference. Do not mix strategies within the same line.

| Strategy | When to use | Example |
|----------|-------------|---------|
| **Effect-equivalent swap** | The reference's function (funny, painful, shocking) matters more than its content | 彩礼 (bride price) → "prenup" or "settlement" — both carry transactional marriage weight |
| **Generalize** | The reference is too niche to swap but the emotion is universal | 八字不合 (incompatible birth charts) → "We were never meant to work" |
| **Preserve + micro-gloss** | The cultural object is visually on screen or plot-critical; viewers need minimal help | 红包 → "red envelope" (self-evident with visual); no further explanation needed |
| **Drop and rewrite** | The reference adds nothing to the plot; removing it loses nothing | Throwaway 内涵段子 meme → rewrite for target-language internet tone |

### Decision rule

1. Is the reference **visible on screen** (prop, costume, setting)? → Preserve + micro-gloss
2. Is it **plot-critical** (drives a decision, conflict, or reveal)? → Effect-equivalent swap with the closest cultural parallel
3. Is it **flavor only** (adds texture but no plot weight)? → Generalize or drop
4. Is it a **punchline**? → Effect-equivalent swap; if no good swap exists, rewrite the joke for the same laugh

## Category 1: Marriage and family customs

Common references:
- **彩礼** (bride price) — transactional weight in marriage negotiation
- **嫁妆** (dowry) — family status display
- **门当户对** (matching family status) — class-based marriage expectation
- **催婚** (pressured to marry) — family nagging about marriage timeline
- **相亲** (arranged blind date) — family-organized matchmaking

Adaptation notes:
- These carry **class, money, and family obligation** energy. In English, swap to "prenup", "family money", "settling down" pressure, or "setup" (for blind dates).
- In Korean/Japanese, equivalent social pressures exist (맞선/お見合い); use the native term directly.
- In Southeast Asian markets, family marriage pressure is broadly relatable; generalize slightly rather than over-explaining.
- In LATAM/Brazil, family involvement in relationships is culturally familiar; keep the warmth but drop China-specific mechanics.

Example:
- "他家彩礼都没给够，还想娶我女儿？" →
  - EN: "He can't even afford the ring and he wants to marry my daughter?"
  - JP: "結納金もまともに出せないくせに、うちの娘と結婚？"
  - KR: "예단도 제대로 못 하면서 우리 딸한테?"

## Category 2: Workplace and hierarchy

Common references:
- **铁饭碗** (iron rice bowl) — secure government/state job
- **996** — 9am-9pm, 6 days a week work culture
- **考公/上岸** (pass civil service exam / "make it to shore") — achieving job security
- **甲方爸爸** (client = "daddy") — sarcastic client worship
- **卷** (involution) — rat-race hyper-competition

Adaptation notes:
- **铁饭碗** → EN: "government job with benefits", "cushy job", "job for life"
- **996** → EN: "working around the clock", "never off the clock" — the number format is meaningless outside China
- **卷** → EN: "the grind", "rat race", "hustle culture" — pick based on whether the tone is bitter, resigned, or mocking
- In Japanese, 社畜 (corporate slave) carries similar energy to 996/卷; use directly
- In Korean, 야근 (overtime culture) and 헬조선 carry comparable weight

## Category 3: Internet slang and meme language

Common references:
- **绿茶 / 绿茶婊** (green tea bitch) — fake-innocent manipulative woman
- **白莲花** (white lotus) — seemingly pure but actually scheming
- **渣男/渣女** (scum man/woman) — player, cheater, heartbreaker
- **舔狗** (simp/lick-dog) — desperate pursuer with no self-respect
- **打脸** (face-slap) — public humiliation reversal
- **吃瓜** (eating melon / watching drama unfold) — spectator enjoying gossip

Adaptation notes:
- These are **character archetypes and social dynamics**, not just slang. Match the social function:
  - 绿茶 → EN: "two-faced", "plays innocent"; not a direct slang swap, rewrite for character read
  - 舔狗 → EN: "simp" works in internet-native contexts; "desperate" for broader audiences
  - 打脸 → EN: "got exposed", "that backfired", "karma" — match the scene's satisfaction level
  - 吃瓜 → EN: "grab the popcorn", "watching this unfold" — match spectator energy
- For card/overlay text, match the target platform's meme energy rather than chasing lexical equivalence

## Category 4: Money, class, and wealth display

Common references:
- **拆迁户** (demolition household) — suddenly rich from property compensation
- **富二代** (rich second generation) — inherited wealth kid
- **凤凰男/凤凰女** (phoenix man/woman) — rural-origin person who climbed to urban elite
- **白富美 / 高富帅** — pretty+rich+fair / tall+rich+handsome ideal types
- **十八线** (18th-tier) — obscure, irrelevant, nobody-level

Adaptation notes:
- **富二代** → EN: "trust fund kid", "old money"; JP: 御曹司; KR: 재벌 2세 — each has a native equivalent
- **凤凰男** → EN: "self-made" (positive) or "from nothing" (neutral) — Chinese term carries class anxiety that needs tone calibration per scene
- **白富美/高富帅** → EN: don't list traits; rewrite as "the total package", "out of everyone's league"
- **十八线** → EN: "a nobody", "Z-list", "who?" — match dismissiveness level

## Category 5: Supernatural, spiritual, and folk belief

Common references:
- **风水** (feng shui) — geomantic luck arrangement
- **八字** (birth chart compatibility) — destiny matching
- **修仙/渡劫** (cultivation / tribulation) — xianxia spiritual progression
- **因果报应** (karmic retribution) — what goes around comes around
- **鬼上身** (ghost possession) — supernatural possession

Adaptation notes:
- **风水** and **karma** are already globally recognized; can preserve directly
- **修仙** terminology: in xianxia-coded content for fandom audiences, preserve pinyin or genre-standard English (e.g., "cultivation", "breakthrough", "tribulation"). For general audiences, generalize to "power up", "level up", "transformation"
- **八字** → EN: "written in the stars", "fate", "destiny" — do not explain the system, capture the fatalism
- For Japanese audiences, 修仙 maps loosely to 修行/仙人 tropes; use native genre vocabulary

## Category 6: Food, festivals, and daily life

Common references:
- **红包** (red envelope) — money gift, now also digital payments
- **摆烂** (lying flat / giving up) — deliberate underachievement
- **内卷** (involution) — overlaps with Category 2
- **年夜饭** (New Year's Eve family dinner) — family reunion obligation
- **坐月子** (postpartum confinement) — traditional post-birth recovery period

Adaptation notes:
- **红包** → if on screen, "red envelope" is self-explanatory. If mentioned in dialogue as "send me a red packet", localize to "send me money", "Venmo me", or equivalent
- **摆烂** → EN: "checked out", "given up", "done trying" — match energy level (defeated vs defiant)
- **年夜饭** → EN: "family dinner" is usually sufficient; the holiday-specific context should come from the scene, not the subtitle
- **坐月子** → EN: this has no Western equivalent; generalize to "recovery" or "resting after the baby" unless the practice itself is the dramatic point

## Usage rule

When diagnosing a line in the localization workflow (Step 2), flag any culture-locked reference and select one of the four adaptation strategies before rewriting. Note the strategy choice in the bilingual output sheet's adjustment column.

If a scene has **3 or more** culture-locked references in a short span, prioritize the plot-critical ones for careful adaptation and generalize or drop the rest. Over-adapting every reference in a dense scene creates subtitle overload.
