# Language Lanes

Use this file when the user wants localization beyond English or wants to compare language markets.

## Default lanes

### English

- Best for widest subtitle-led export testing
- Prefer clean, globally legible spoken lines
- Avoid translationese and over-the-top literal dominance language

Pitfalls:
- "You dare?" / "This matter is not over" / "You think you won?" are classic translationese traps from Chinese; rewrite completely
- False friends from Chinese loan-translations (e.g. "eat vinegar" for jealousy) must be replaced, not explained
- Possessive/dominance language ("you are mine", "obey me") escalates much faster in English than in Chinese context

### Spanish

- Keep emotional directness
- Allow slightly warmer or more expressive phrasing than English
- Avoid slang that is too country-specific unless the user wants LATAM-specific or Spain-specific wording

Pitfalls:
- **tu / vos / usted** varies drastically by region (Spain vs Mexico vs Argentina); wrong choice breaks immersion
- Gendered adjective endings (-o/-a) can shift character perception; be deliberate with gender-ambiguous characters
- LATAM and Spain Spanish differ significantly in profanity intensity — the same word can be mild in one and severe in the other

### Japanese

- Prioritize tone fit and character register
- Avoid over-literal transfer from Chinese phrasing
- Use this lane when the project is anime-coded or the user wants fandom-native delivery

Pitfalls:
- **Keigo register** (desu/masu polite vs tameguchi casual) directly encodes character relationships; wrong register breaks the scene entirely
- Honorific suffixes (-san, -kun, -sama, -chan) carry relationship weight; do not omit or misapply
- Chinese and Japanese share kanji but meanings often diverge; do not assume cognate equivalence
- Sentence-final particles (ne, yo, wa, ze) define character personality; choose deliberately

### Korean

- Keep power dynamics legible and socially precise
- Works well for cold, sharp, status-heavy dialogue
- Avoid making everything sound like K-drama parody

Pitfalls:
- **Banmal (반말) vs jondaenmal (존댓말)** choice directly encodes power and status; mixing them signals deliberate disrespect
- Age-based speech levels are non-negotiable in Korean social context; a younger character using banmal to an elder is a story event, not a translation choice
- Sino-Korean vocabulary (한자어) can sound overly formal; balance with native Korean words for natural dialogue

### Thai

- Prioritize natural emotional readability
- Keep humor and teasing light-footed
- Avoid over-complication in subtitle timing

Pitfalls:
- **Pronouns carry intimacy, status, and gender** simultaneously; the wrong pronoun choice changes the entire relationship read
- Sentence-final particles (kha/khrap/ka/krap) signal gender and politeness; omitting them sounds rude or robotic
- Thai has no spaces between words, making subtitle line-breaking more complex; break at phrase boundaries

### Indonesian

- Prefer clear, contemporary spoken phrasing
- Keep lines compact and accessible
- Good for broad subtitle consumption and lighter tonal material

Pitfalls:
- Formal/informal register is less stratified than other Asian languages, but **loanword origin** (Arabic-origin vs Dutch-origin vs English-origin) carries subtle cultural signals
- Aku/saya/gue pronoun choice signals formality and regional flavor (gue is Jakarta casual)
- Religious references land differently in Indonesia's diverse population; be cautious with supernatural/religious localization choices

### Vietnamese

- Preserve speed and emotional clarity
- Avoid stiff formal constructions unless status is important
- Good for high-energy short-form dialogue

Pitfalls:
- **Pronoun system (anh/em/chi/toi/con/ong/ba)** simultaneously encodes age, gender, intimacy, and social status; getting it wrong is immediately jarring to native speakers
- Vietnamese tonal writing with diacritics must be preserved correctly in subtitles; missing diacritics change word meaning entirely
- Northern vs Southern dialect differences affect vocabulary and particle usage

### Portuguese (Brazil)

- Allows expressive rhythm and emotional swing
- Keep it natural and contemporary
- Avoid slang overload unless the user explicitly wants high-local flavor

Pitfalls:
- **Voce / tu** distinction varies by region within Brazil; Rio uses tu casually, Sao Paulo defaults to voce
- Brazilian Portuguese slang differs significantly from European Portuguese; using the wrong variant breaks immersion for the target audience
- Diminutives (-inho/-inha) are extremely common in casual speech and can signal affection, sarcasm, or dismissal depending on context

## Lane selection rule

If the user asks for multiple languages, keep the same source diagnosis and risk logic, but localize each lane according to its own natural rhythm and social tone.
