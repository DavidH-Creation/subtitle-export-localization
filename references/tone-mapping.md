# Tone Mapping

Use this file when the scene depends on tone rather than literal wording.

## Threats

Chinese dialogue often states intent directly. In English, stronger threats are often shorter and colder.

Examples:
- "你死定了" -> "You're done."
- "这事没完" -> "This isn't over."
- "你敢动她试试" -> "Touch her and see what happens."

## Flirtation

Chinese flirtation is often indirect or teasing. In English, keep chemistry but avoid accidental corniness.

Examples:
- "你是不是喜欢我" -> "You always act like this when you're interested?"
- "嘴这么硬" -> "You always this defensive?"

## Humiliation and taunts

Replace literal insults with socially legible English humiliation.

Examples:
- "你就是个笑话" -> "You're embarrassing yourself."
- "小丑" -> "This is pathetic." or "You're a joke."

## Bossy or possessive lines

These are high-risk in English because they can turn silly or toxic too fast.

Guideline:
- Keep tension.
- Reduce accidental parody.
- Use subtext when possible.

Example:
- "我是你的主人" can be localized by context as:
  - "Look at me. Stay with me."
  - "You answer to me."
  - "I'm the one pulling you back."

Choose based on scene tone, not literal wording.

## Meme and internet energy

Do not chase word-for-word meme equivalence. Match the social effect:
- mocking
- cringe-calling
- thirst
- fandom-coded exaggeration

If a direct meme equivalent feels dated, use neutral internet-native English instead.

## Narration cards and end tags

Do not translate subtitle cards or episode end tags as if they were ordinary spoken lines.

Examples:
- "已捕获S级供暖设备 x1" should be treated as a punchline card, not a literal inventory note
- "动了不该动的东西？学生会执法队正在介入……" should read like a hook card for viewers

Guideline:
- Keep them short
- Keep them clicky or dramatic when needed
- Preserve episode-end hook energy over literal wording
